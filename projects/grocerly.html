Overview

What Grocerly is and what I contributed.

What it is

  • Meal planning + grocery support designed around weekly routines.
  • Calendar integration for planning meals across days.
  • Pantry tracking to reduce duplicate buying and food waste.

What I did

  • UX research + ideation (SCAMPER, brain mapping)
  • Wireframes → prototype iteration
  • Usability/accessibility improvements based on feedback

Design goal

Make it easier to answer: “What am I eating this week?” while supporting dietary constraints and reducing planning effort.

Problem

Why meal planning feels hard — and what users need instead.

Planning friction

Pain point

Meal planning is often scattered across notes, apps, and memory. Users lose track of what they planned and what they already have.

Cognitive load • Visibility of system status

Dietary constraints

Need

Filtering for allergies, preferences, or restrictions should be built into the experience, not an afterthought.

Error prevention • Personalization

Pantry awareness

Opportunity

Users buy duplicates or forget ingredients. Pantry tracking can reduce waste and improve confidence when planning meals.

Recognition over recall

Process

A quick look at how we moved from ideation to prototype.

1) Ideation
Brain mapping + SCAMPER to generate features and workflows.
2) Wireframes
Low-fidelity screens mapping the end-to-end weekly planning flow.
3) Prototype
Clickable interactions for planner, meal cards, pantry, and filters.
4) Iteration
Refinements based on feedback: clarity, navigation, and accessibility.

Solution

Key interaction ideas and what makes Grocerly feel usable.

Key features

  • Weekly planner with one-tap meal placement
  • Meal cards with clear ingredients + dietary tags
  • Allergy/restriction filters
  • Pantry list with “already have” visibility

UX principles

  • Reduce recall: show pantry + planned meals side-by-side
  • Make state visible: what’s planned, what’s missing, what’s available
  • Error prevention: restrictions filter before planning

Reflection

What I learned and what I’d improve next.

What I learned

  • Meal planning is less about features and more about reducing mental load.
  • Small clarity improvements (labels, structure) strongly affect confidence.
  • Dietary support needs to be “first-class,” not hidden.

Next improvements

  • Add onboarding for first-time setup (diet, pantry, goals).
  • Test planner interactions with real routines (busy weeks).
  • Add accessibility checks (focus states, contrast, keyboard nav).
